Module 1: Understanding the Phishing Threat

Goal

To equip every employee with the skills to instantly recognize, analyze, and safely report a phishing attempt, making them the organization's first line of defense.

1. What is Phishing?

Phishing is a form of social engineering where an attacker attempts to trick you into clicking a malicious link, downloading a malicious file, or voluntarily giving up sensitive information (like passwords or credit card numbers).

It is named after the fishing technique: attackers throw out digital "bait" hoping someone "bites."

Types of Phishing

TypeDescription
Email PhishingBroad, non-targeted attacks sent to millions of people (e.g., a fake prize notification).
Spear PhishingHighly targeted attacks aimed at specific individuals or companies (e.g., an email appearing to come from your CEO or HR department).
VishingVoice Phishing. Uses phone calls (often automated) to trick users into giving information.
SmishingSMS Phishing. Uses text messages to deliver malicious links or numbers to call.

2. The Attacker's Playbook: Common Red Flags (TTPs)

Phishing emails always follow a pattern designed to bypass your rational thought process. Learn to spot these Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs):

A. The Pressure Cooker (Urgency & Threat)

Attackers thrive on panic. They create a false sense of urgency to make you act before you think.

Red Flag: Phrases like "Immediate action required," "Your account will be suspended in 2 hours," or "Final warning before legal action."

Defense: Legitimate organizations rarely use threats or unreasonable deadlines to communicate critical technical issues. If it feels urgent, pause and verify.

B. The Identity Deception (Sender Spoofing)

The "From" name might look familiar (like a colleague or vendor), but the technical details are wrong.

Red Flag: The sender's display name is correct (e.g., "Accounting Dept"), but the underlying email address is gibberish, ends in a public domain (@gmail.com), or has a subtle typo (e.g., Micros0ft.com instead of Microsoft.com).

Defense: Always check the full email address. On mobile devices, tap the sender's name to reveal the actual address.

C. The Hidden Destination (Malicious Links)

The text of the link might say "Click here for login portal," but the actual destination is a different, dangerous site.

Red Flag: The link text looks like a legitimate domain, but when you hover your cursor over it, the pop-up address displayed at the bottom of the screen is completely different or contains suspicious characters.

Defense: NEVER click a link if you haven't checked the destination first. On a touch screen, you can often "long-press" (press and hold) the link to preview the URL without navigating to it.

D. The Unprofessional Look

Phishing messages are often mass-produced and contain errors.

Red Flag: Obvious spelling mistakes, poor grammar, weird formatting, or low-resolution logos.

Defense: While professional phishing exists, look for consistent quality. If an email from a major bank looks like it was written quickly, it's likely a scam.

3. The Defender's Playbook: Three Actionable Steps

If a message is suspicious, follow these three steps in order:

Stop and Hover

Do NOT click any links or open any attachments.

Hover your mouse over the sender's name and all links to inspect the true destination and email address.

Verify Out-of-Band (The Golden Rule)

If the email is requesting an action (like a wire transfer, password change, or urgent file download), you must verify the request using a communication channel separate from the email itself.

Do this: Call the person or department using a known, pre-saved phone number from the company directory, not a number provided in the suspicious email.

Report Immediately

Use the official company-provided "Report Phishing" button or follow the internal security procedure.

Reporting the email allows the security team to investigate and remove the threat from other users' inboxes.

Crucial Warning

If you accidentally enter your credentials on a malicious page, immediately change your password on the legitimate site and report the incident to the security team without delay.

Learning outcomes

  • Recognize the most common phishing techniques and social-engineering patterns.
  • Apply a short, repeatable verification workflow to reduce risk (Stop, Verify, Report).
  • Report suspected phishing promptly and provide useful context to the security team.
  • Complete a short assessment to demonstrate retention and readiness.

Exercises (classroom / self-study)

Use these short exercises to reinforce learning. They are designed for a 20–40 minute classroom session or a self-paced lab.

  • Spot the phish: Review 6 sample emails (mix of benign and malicious). Mark each as safe or suspicious, note the red flags, and submit a short justification.
  • Link inspection: Practice previewing links on desktop and mobile; capture screenshots of the displayed destination and explain why it is suspicious or safe.
  • Reporting drill: Use the company's 'Report Phishing' button (or forward to security) and include the required fields: sender, subject, time, and why you flagged it.

Instructor notes

  • Run simulated phishing campaigns with graduated difficulty and provide immediate, constructive feedback to learners.
  • De-brief every exercise by highlighting the most useful red flags and showing the artifacts (headers, URLs, IP lookups) used during analysis.
  • Protect learner privacy: when running class-wide simulations, report aggregate results and avoid singling out individuals in public settings.
